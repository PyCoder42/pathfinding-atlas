The route from “correct” to “instant”

A continent has hundreds of millions of road segments. Plain shortest-path search is correct but far too slow for a live query. Here's how routers close the gap — the same path this site lets you watch step by step.

  1. 1

    Explore outward — Dijkstra

    Dijkstra's algorithm fans out from the start, always expanding the cheapest-so-far node. It always finds the shortest route, but explores in every direction — millions of nodes for a long trip.

  2. 2

    Aim at the goal — A*

    A* adds a heuristic — a straight-line lower bound on the remaining time — so the search leans toward the destination instead of spreading evenly. Same optimal route, far fewer nodes touched.

  3. 3

    Skip the boring roads — Contraction Hierarchies

    Most of a long trip is highways. CH preprocesses the map once into shortcuts that jump over unimportant streets, so a live query checks a few hundred nodes instead of millions. This is the core trick in real routers.

  4. 4

    Keep up with traffic — Customizable CH

    When live traffic changes every edge weight, rebuilding from scratch is too slow. CCH separates the structure from the metric, so it re-customizes in milliseconds — which is how your ETA stays current.

Used by Google Maps

Contraction Hierarchies and its customizable variant (CCH) are what production routers like Google Maps and OSRM actually run. On this site they carry the Maps badge — select them and watch them leave Dijkstra behind.

Weighted & unweighted graphs

Two labs in one. Unweighted: BFS, DFS and Bidirectional BFS on mazes and grids — fewest steps. Weighted: Dijkstra → A* → CH → CCH on terrain, huge meshes, even negative weights — lowest cost, at scale.
